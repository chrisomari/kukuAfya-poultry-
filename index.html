DISEASES

1. Newcastle Disease (ND)

Symptoms:
  • Respiratory distress (gasping, coughing)
  • Greenish diarrhea
  • Nervous signs (tremors, twisted neck)
  • Reduced egg production and abnormal eggs
  • High mortality rate in severe outbreaks
Prevention:

Vaccinate regularly, limit visitors, disinfect equipment, and manage wild bird exposure.

Treatment:

No specific treatment; focus on vaccination and supportive care.

2. Marek’s Disease

Symptoms:
  • Paralysis in legs, wings, or neck
  • Tumors in internal organs
  • Vision impairment
  • Weight loss and drop in egg production
Prevention:

Vaccination at hatchery, maintain good biosecurity, avoid introducing infected birds, and minimize stress.

Treatment:

No cure; prevention through vaccination and biosecurity is crucial.

3. Avian Influenza (Bird Flu)

Symptoms:
  • Severe respiratory distress (gasping, coughing)
  • Swelling of the head and neck
  • Purple discoloration of comb and wattles
  • Sudden death in severe cases
Prevention:

Strict biosecurity to keep wild birds and other poultry away. Avoid movement of poultry and equipment from infected areas.

Treatment:

No effective treatment; infected flocks may be culled to prevent spread. Prevention through biosecurity is essential.

4. Infectious Bronchitis (IB)

Symptoms:
  • Respiratory issues (sneezing, coughing)
  • Drop in egg production and poor eggshell quality
  • Watery diarrhea
Prevention:

Vaccination, especially if prevalent in the area. Good biosecurity practices, including proper ventilation and isolation of new birds.

Treatment:

No specific treatment; supportive care, hydration, and hygiene management are key.

5. Coccidiosis

Symptoms:
  • Bloody or watery droppings
  • Lethargy and ruffled feathers
  • Loss of appetite and dehydration
  • Reduced growth rate in young birds
Prevention:

Clean, dry litter to reduce parasite buildup, prophylactic anticoccidial medications, and vaccination where available.

Treatment:

Anticoccidial drugs as per vet instructions. Maintain cleanliness to limit spread.

6. Fowl Cholera

Symptoms:
  • Swollen wattles and face
  • Greenish diarrhea
  • Lameness due to joint infections
  • Sudden death in acute cases
Prevention:

Vaccination in endemic areas, avoid overcrowding, and maintain cleanliness.

Treatment:

Antibiotics as prescribed; focus on prevention through hygiene and biosecurity.

7. Infectious Bursal Disease (IBD or Gumboro)

Symptoms:
  • Ruffled feathers and lethargy
  • Diarrhea, often whitish
  • Swollen cloaca
  • High mortality in young chickens
Prevention:

Vaccination for young chicks, clean and disinfected environment.

Treatment:

No specific treatment; provide supportive care to manage symptoms.

General Preventative Tips for All Diseases

Vaccination Program:

Develop a consistent vaccination schedule to protect against the most common diseases.

Cleanliness:

Regularly clean and disinfect housing, feeding, and watering equipment.

Biosecurity:

Limit exposure to other birds, wild birds, and people who may carry pathogens.

Proper Nutrition:

A balanced diet strengthens immune responses.

Ventilation and Housing:

Ensure good airflow and minimize overcrowding to reduce stress.

BEAKTRIMMING

Beak trimming is the removal of part of the top and bottom beak of a bird. It is also called “debeaking”, although this term is inaccurate as only part of the beak is removed. It is an animal husbandry practice commonly carried out in the poultry industry. Farm managers have their flocks beak-trimmed to blunt the beaks enough to prevent the occurrence of damaging pecking. Re-trimming may also be carried out if a bird’s beak grows back enough to cause pecking damage. Birds are often re-trimmed at 8–12 weeks of age to avoid this happening. Some non-trimmed adult birds may need trimming if a pecking outbreak occurs. A beak trimmed bird Why is beak trimming done? Beak trimming is performed early in the life of commercial hens to decrease injuries caused by cannibalism, bullying, and feather and vent pecking. Birds naturally peck at the environment and each other to investigate and work out where they fit into the flock (pecking order). This behaviour can become a problem in commercial situations and many deaths have been recorded among untrimmed hens. Feather pecking and cannibalism affects all birds in all production systems. When laying birds are kept in systems that give the opportunity for aggressive birds to contact many other birds, cannibalism and feather pecking can spread rapidly through the flock and result in injuries and mortality. Mortality of up to 25–30% of the flock can occur and cause huge mortality and morbidity problems as well as financial losses to the farmer. VACCINATION

Importance of Vaccination

Vaccination is crucial in preventing various diseases in chickens, ensuring their health and productivity. Effective vaccination programs can reduce mortality rates and improve the overall well-being of the flock.

Common Vaccinations for Chickens

  • Marek's Disease: Vaccinate at hatch or one day old.
  • Newcastle Disease: Administer at 2-3 weeks, with a booster at 6-8 weeks.
  • Infectious Bronchitis: Given at 3-6 weeks, especially in layers.
  • Coccidiosis: Administer through water or feed starting at 1-3 weeks.
  • Avian Encephalomyelitis: Vaccinate at 4-8 weeks.

Vaccination Schedule

Age (Weeks) Vaccine Notes
1 Marek's Disease Given at hatch or one day old.
2-3 Newcastle Disease First vaccination, booster at 6-8 weeks.
3-6 Infectious Bronchitis Especially important for layers.
1-3 Coccidiosis Can be given through water or feed.
4-8 Avian Encephalomyelitis Administer during this period.

General Vaccination Tips

  • Always follow the manufacturer's instructions for each vaccine.
  • Maintain a clean and stress-free environment during vaccination.
  • Consult with a veterinarian for a tailored vaccination program based on your flock's needs.
  • Keep accurate records of vaccinations to monitor flock health.

NUTRITION

Here's a comprehensive guide to chicken nutrition, covering essential nutrients and feed options available for different growth stages and purposes. Good nutrition is crucial to support growth, egg production, and overall health in chickens.

Essential Nutrients for Chickens

Protein:
  • Vital for muscle and tissue development, especially in young birds.
  • Protein levels vary by age: chicks need around 18-20% protein, while adult layers need about 16-18%.
Carbohydrates:
  • Provide energy to support activity, growth, and production.
  • Primarily sourced from grains such as corn and wheat.
Fats:
  • Supply additional energy and are necessary for absorbing fat-soluble vitamins.
  • Common sources include vegetable oils and certain seeds.
Vitamins:
  • Essential vitamins include A, D, E, K, B12, and riboflavin for immunity, bone health, and overall vitality.
  • A good premix feed often includes these in balanced amounts.
Minerals:
  • Calcium and phosphorus are critical, especially for laying hens who need calcium for eggshell production.
  • Trace minerals like iron, magnesium, and zinc support various bodily functions.
Water:
  • Fresh, clean water is vital and should always be available.

Specialty Feeds

Options:
  • Organic Feed: Brands like Scratch and Peck or Kalmbach Feeds offer certified organic options.
  • Supplemental Treats: Scratch grains, mealworms, and cracked corn can be offered occasionally in moderation.

Homemade Chicken Feed Options

If you prefer to make your own feed, ensure it includes a balanced mix of grains, protein sources, and supplements:

  • Base Grains: Corn, wheat, barley, and oats.
  • Protein Sources: Soybean meal, fish meal, or dried insects.
  • Mineral & Vitamin Supplements: Include calcium carbonate (for egg-laying hens), vitamin D powder, and a trace mineral mix.
Example Mix:

For a balanced homemade feed, use a mix of 50% corn, 30% soybean meal, and 20% oats, with added supplements.

Additional Feeding Tips

  • Grit: Grit helps chickens grind up their food and is essential if they’re consuming whole grains. Offer it free-choice in a separate dish.
  • Oyster Shells: Provide a separate dish of oyster shells for laying hens to support strong eggshells.
  • Probiotics and Supplements: Some chicken feeds contain probiotics for gut health or additional supplements for immune support. You can add these separately if needed.

Feeding Recommendations

  • Quantity: Adult chickens typically consume about 1/4 pound (120 grams) of feed per day. Adjust depending on breed, size, and season.
  • Timing: Feed in the morning and let them graze or free-range if possible. Offer more feed if they have limited access to natural forage.
  • Storage: Keep feed in a dry, cool place in airtight containers to prevent mold and pests.

Recommended Brands and Purchase Options

  • Nature’s Best Organic: Organic options ideal for all-natural poultry raising.
  • Manna Pro: Offers medicated and non-medicated options for chicks and layers, plus supplemental treats.
  • Scratch and Peck: Great for those interested in organic and whole-grain feeds.

Tip

A well-balanced diet tailored to the age and purpose of your chickens is essential for their health and productivity. From high-protein starter feeds for chicks to calcium-rich layer feeds for egg production, choosing the right feed at each stage supports a strong, healthy flock.

Environmental Housing for Chickens

1. Housing Types

  • Chicken Coops: Enclosed structures that provide shelter from weather and predators.
  • Free-Range Systems: Allow chickens to roam outdoors while having access to a coop.
  • Pastured Systems: Movable coops (chicken tractors) are moved to fresh pasture regularly.

2. Key Considerations for Housing

  • Ventilation: Adequate airflow is crucial to prevent overheating and reduce humidity.
  • Insulation: Helps maintain stable temperatures in the coop.
  • Space Requirements: 2-3 square feet per bird inside the coop; 8-10 square feet outdoors.
  • Lighting: Natural or artificial lighting should ensure 14-16 hours of light for laying hens.

3. Environmental Enrichment

  • Perches: Install at varying heights for natural roosting behavior.
  • Nesting Boxes: 1 box for every 3-4 hens for secure laying areas.
  • Dust Baths: Allow access to dust baths for feather maintenance and parasite control.

4. Materials for Housing

  • Construction Materials: Use non-toxic materials like treated wood, metal, or concrete.
  • Flooring Options: Dirt, concrete, or wood shavings, with proper drainage.

5. Predator Protection

  • Fencing: Secure fencing to protect against raccoons, foxes, etc.
  • Coop Security: Well-sealed doors and windows; use hardware cloth for better protection.

6. Waste Management

  • Litter Management: Use bedding material to absorb waste; regular cleaning is essential.
  • Composting: Chicken manure can be composted for garden use.

7. Water Supply

  • Clean Water Access: Provide fresh water at all times with easy-to-clean drinkers.

8. Temperature Regulation

  • Heating: Use heat lamps or pads for warmth in colder climates.
  • Cooling: Provide shade and consider misters or fans in hot weather.

9. Monitoring Health and Behavior

  • Behavioral Observations: Regularly check for signs of stress, illness, or bullying.
  • Biosecurity Measures: Implement cleanliness measures to prevent disease transmission.

Conclusion

A well-planned housing environment is crucial for the health and productivity of chickens. By addressing factors like space, ventilation, safety, and enrichment, you can ensure a thriving flock.

WASTE MANAGEMENT

INTRODUCTION

Types of Waste:

  • Manure: Solid and liquid waste produced by chickens.
  • Bedding Material: Used bedding that absorbs moisture and waste.
  • Feed Waste: Leftover feed that is not consumed.
  • Dead Birds: Carcasses that need proper disposal.

Importance of Waste Management:

  • Reduces environmental pollution.
  • Prevents the spread of diseases.
  • Minimizes odors and pests.
  • Enhances farm hygiene and animal welfare.

Waste Management Practices:

  • Composting: Decomposing manure and bedding to create nutrient-rich fertilizer.
  • Anaerobic Digestion: Using bacteria to break down organic waste in the absence of oxygen to produce biogas.
  • Litter Management: Regularly changing bedding material to maintain a clean environment.
  • Proper Disposal: Safe disposal of dead birds through incineration, burial, or rendering.

Benefits of Effective Waste Management:

  • Reduces the need for chemical fertilizers by recycling nutrients.
  • Improves soil health and productivity when using compost.
  • Reduces greenhouse gas emissions from decomposing waste.
  • Promotes a sustainable farming practice.

Regulatory Compliance:

  • Adhering to local and national regulations regarding waste disposal and management.

Monitoring and Record-Keeping:

  • Keeping detailed records of waste production and management practices.
